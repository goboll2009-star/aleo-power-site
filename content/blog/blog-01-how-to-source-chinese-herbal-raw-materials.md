## Introduction

Sourcing Chinese herbal raw materials for international B2B supply is not the same as buying finished retail products online. A buyer needs to understand the herb origin, processing form, moisture level, appearance, batch documents, packaging, export requirements and supplier communication process before moving from sample to bulk order.

For importers, supplement brands, TCM clinics, herbal wholesalers and private label teams, the main risk is usually not only price. The bigger questions are: Will the supplier deliver the same quality after the first sample? Can they prepare the documents required by the buyer's market? Can they control moisture, foreign matter, pesticide residues, heavy metals and microbiological risks? Can packaging survive international transport?

This guide explains how a professional buyer can evaluate a Chinese herbal raw material supplier in a practical, procurement-focused way. It is written for B2B buyers who need reliable herbs such as ginseng, astragalus, angelica, licorice, codonopsis, ginger, cinnamon, goji berry and other traditional Chinese herbal ingredients.

## What Are Chinese Herbal Raw Materials in B2B Trade?

Chinese herbal raw materials are unprocessed or minimally processed plant materials used by manufacturers, wholesalers, clinics, tea brands, extract producers and herbal product companies. They may include roots, rhizomes, fruits, seeds, flowers, leaves, bark or fungi, depending on the product.

In B2B trade, the buyer usually cares about:

- Botanical identity
- Chinese and English product name
- Plant part used
- Harvest origin
- Processing method
- Cutting or slicing size
- Moisture level
- Foreign matter control
- Packaging format
- Available testing documents
- Export documentation
- Shelf life and storage condition

Unlike consumer-facing herbal content, a procurement page should not focus on disease treatment claims. It should focus on product definition, specification control and safe commercial handling.

## H2: Start With the Correct Product Identity

The first step is to confirm that buyer and supplier are discussing the same material. Many herbs have similar English names, regional names or processing forms. A buyer should request the botanical name, plant part and processing form in writing.

### H3: Ask for Botanical Name and Plant Part

For example, a buyer may ask for "ginseng," but there are different botanical sources and processing styles. A responsible supplier should be able to clarify the product name, botanical identity, origin and whether the product is raw, sliced, powdered or extracted.

Practical RFQ fields include:

- Product name
- Botanical name
- Plant part used
- Grade or specification
- Processing form
- Intended application
- Required documents
- Destination country

This prevents misunderstandings at the sample stage and reduces the risk of receiving a material that does not fit the buyer's production process.

## H2: Evaluate the Origin and Harvest Logic

Many Chinese herbal materials are associated with specific growing regions. Origin does not automatically guarantee quality, but it helps buyers understand supply stability, harvest season, appearance and price structure.

For example, goji berry buyers may ask about Ningxia origin, while astragalus or codonopsis buyers may request information about growing areas and harvest timing. A supplier should avoid vague statements such as "best quality" and instead provide specific origin and batch information where available.

### H3: Useful Origin Questions

Ask the supplier:

- Which province or growing region is this batch from?
- Is the material wild-collected or cultivated?
- What is the normal harvest season?
- Is the material available year-round?
- Can the origin be marked on documents or labels if required?

If the supplier cannot answer basic origin questions, the buyer should be cautious before moving to a large order.

## H2: Define the Specification Before Discussing Price

Price is meaningful only when the specification is clear. A lower price may come from smaller size, higher moisture, mixed grade, less uniform cutting, different origin or weaker documentation.

For bulk Chinese herbal raw materials, useful specification items include:

- Whole, sliced, cut, powdered or granulated form
- Size range or slice thickness
- Moisture target
- Foreign matter limit
- Color and appearance expectation
- Odor and taste description where relevant
- Packing size per bag or carton
- Shelf life
- Storage instruction

### H3: Why Specification Control Matters

If a herbal tea brand needs visible, clean and uniform slices, appearance may be critical. If an extract manufacturer will process the herb further, active marker content, moisture and foreign matter may be more important. If a distributor sells to clinics, consistent cutting and clean packaging may affect customer satisfaction.

The buyer should tell the supplier the application instead of only asking for "best price." A good supplier can recommend a suitable grade after understanding the use case.

## H2: Review COA and Testing Requirements

A Certificate of Analysis, or COA, is one of the most important documents in B2B herbal ingredient sourcing. It should not be treated as decoration. It helps the buyer understand what was tested, which batch was tested and whether the result matches the buyer's market requirements.

Common test categories may include:

- Identity or appearance
- Moisture
- Ash
- Heavy metals
- Pesticide residues
- Microbiological limits
- Marker compounds where applicable

Testing requirements vary by product, application and market. A supplier should not promise that every herb automatically meets every market standard without checking the exact product and requirement.

### H3: COA Questions to Ask

Before ordering, ask:

- Is the COA batch-specific?
- Which tests are included?
- Can third-party testing be arranged?
- Is the testing method stated?
- Can the supplier provide a sample COA before production?
- Can the final batch be inspected before shipment?

This conversation gives the buyer a realistic view of quality control and avoids over-promising.

## H2: Request a Sample and Compare It With Bulk Delivery

Samples are useful, but they can also be misleading if the buyer does not document what was approved. A sample should be photographed, labeled and connected to a written specification.

When the sample arrives, evaluate:

- Appearance
- Color
- Odor
- Slice uniformity
- Foreign matter
- Moisture feel
- Packaging condition
- Label clarity

For repeat orders, keep a retained sample or clear photo record. During shipment inspection, compare the bulk batch against the approved sample and agreed specification.

## H2: Understand MOQ, Lead Time and Seasonal Supply

MOQ depends on product type, processing form, packaging requirement and market availability. Raw herbs may have different MOQs from sliced herbs, powders or private label packs. Some products are more seasonal, while others can be stocked more consistently.

Buyers should ask:

- What is the MOQ for standard bulk packing?
- What is the MOQ for custom packing?
- What is the sample lead time?
- What is the bulk order lead time?
- Is the product seasonal?
- What happens if the next batch differs from the sample?

Clear answers help the buyer plan inventory and avoid last-minute purchasing pressure.

## H2: Check Packaging and Export Preparation

Herbal raw materials are sensitive to moisture, odor transfer and packaging damage. Export packing should protect the material during storage, loading and international transport.

Common options include:

- Inner plastic bag plus outer woven bag
- Kraft bag
- Carton packing
- Vacuum packing for selected products
- Private label retail pack
- Palletizing if required

The buyer should confirm net weight, gross weight, carton size, label content, storage instruction and whether the supplier can prepare packing lists and commercial invoices.

### H3: Shipment Inspection Checklist

Before shipment, inspect:

- Product name and batch number
- Bag or carton condition
- Net weight per package
- Label accuracy
- Moisture-sensitive packing
- Carton sealing
- Pallet condition if used
- Photos of loaded goods

This is especially important for first orders and private label projects.

## H2: Avoid Over-Reliance on Lowest Price

In herbal raw material sourcing, the lowest quote can create hidden costs. Poor sorting, inconsistent cutting, weak packaging, unclear documents or delayed shipment can cost more than a small price difference.

A buyer should compare suppliers using a weighted evaluation:

- Product identity clarity
- Sample quality
- Specification control
- Testing ability
- Communication speed
- Export experience
- Packaging reliability
- Price stability
- Lead time

This approach supports long-term procurement instead of one-time bargain hunting.

## FAQ

### What documents should I request when buying Chinese herbal raw materials?

Common documents include COA, packing list, commercial invoice, product specification sheet and shipment photos. Depending on the product and market, third-party test reports or additional documents may be needed.

### Is a sample always the same as the bulk order?

Not automatically. Buyers should connect the sample to an agreed specification and request final batch photos or inspection before shipment.

### What is the difference between raw herbs and herbal slices?

Raw herbs are usually whole or minimally processed materials. Herbal slices are cut or processed into more uniform forms for clinics, retail, tea blending or further manufacturing.

### Can Chinese herbal raw materials be used for dietary supplements?

Some materials may be used in supplement manufacturing, but suitability depends on product identity, regulation, testing, specification and the buyer's formulation requirements. Buyers should confirm local regulatory requirements.

### How can I reduce quality risk in the first order?

Start with a clear specification, request a sample, review COA requirements, confirm packing details and ask for shipment inspection photos before dispatch.

## Conclusion

Sourcing Chinese herbal raw materials for B2B supply requires more than finding a supplier with a low price. Buyers should confirm product identity, origin, specification, testing, sample approval, packaging and shipment preparation before placing a bulk order.

The strongest supplier relationship is built on clear communication and realistic documentation. A professional supplier should help the buyer understand what can be controlled, what must be tested and what should be confirmed before shipment.
